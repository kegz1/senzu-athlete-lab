Archery involves complex isometric stabilization patterns requiring exceptional postural endurance, scapular control, and rotational stability. Research by Ertan et al. (2011) demonstrates that elite archers exhibit distinct muscle activation patterns, particularly in the forearm flexors and extensors, with a characteristic "relaxation-contraction-relaxation" pattern during the release phase.   • Deadbug w/ Reach - 2 sets x 10 reps/side

Exercise Focus: Wall Slides

Execution: Stand with back against wall, arms in "W" position. Slide arms up wall while maintaining contact with lower back, elbows, and wrists. Reach full overhead position, then return to start.

Scientific Basis: Research by Kibler et al. (2013) demonstrates that wall slides effectively activate the lower trapezius and serratus anterior‚Äîkey muscles for optimal scapular positioning during the archery draw phase.

Technical Cues: "Maintain three points of contact," "Pull shoulder blades down and back," "Keep ribs down"

Main Workout:

  • Half-Kneeling Single Arm Cable Row - 3 sets x 10 reps/side
  • Bottoms-Up KB Press - 3 sets x 8 reps/side
  • Plank with Arm Reach - 3 sets x 30 seconds
  • Banded Archer Rows (Pause 3 sec) - 3 sets x 12 reps

Finisher:

  • Single Arm Farmer's Carry - 3 sets x 30 meters

Exercise Focus: Pallof Press

Execution: Stand perpendicular to band anchor point, hold band at chest with both hands. Press band straight out and hold, resisting rotation. Return to chest and repeat.

Scientific Basis: Research by McGill et al. (2009) demonstrates that the Pallof press effectively activates the transverse abdominis and oblique muscles in an anti-rotation pattern that directly mimics the core stabilization demands of the archery shot cycle.
